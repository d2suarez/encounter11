STUDENT ASSESSMENT

SUMMATIVE ASSESSMENT OPTIONS:

  1. Participate in a Night of the Notables, where you will assume the character of a key person in the Reformation and look and act like and answer questions about the life of that person. Key people include Huldrych Zwingli (Switzerland), John Wycliffe (England), Hugo Jan Huss (Czech), Jerome of Prague (Czech), Martin Luther (Germany), William Farel (France), John Calvin (France), Louis de Berquin (France), Menno Simons (Netherlands), Hans Tausen (Denmark), Olaf and Laurentius Petri (Sweden), William Tyndale (England), John Knox (Scotland), Charles Wesley (England). You may also choose to include others such as Johann Tetzel and the cardinal at the Council of Trent, and other historical figures from the papal side.

    Create 10 question cards for people to take who may want to engage in conversation. You should also be prepared to answer any personal questions that may arise from visitors on the character and in particular, on the role of the Bible at that time. Invite the school community (or simply parents of students in the class) to participate in this event.

  2. Create a character profile on one of the characters mentioned in option number 1. Record facts, traits, properties, or qualities about your chosen Reformer. To summarize their learning, create a summary paragraph about how the individual contributed to the endurance of the Bible as well as a descriptive paragraph about the person’s life and what they contributed to the Reformation. Then decorate your character profiles using natural resources or other symbolic objects.

  3. Write a short story from the perspective of one of the Reformers, focusing on the role of the Bible in the Reformer’s life.

  4. Respond to the following questions as short answer responses during class time:

    • How do you feel about the Reformers and others who literally gave their lives so that the Bible could be translated into the common languages?

    • What is so powerful about the Bible?

    • Why, do you think, were the church leaders so opposed to the Bible being translated into common languages?

    • Today, in what ways do we show we value the Bible?

  5. Identify and give examples of how the Bible has been “a lamp to your feet, and a light to your path,” recommending how it could benefit someone who doesn’t believe the Bible to be trustworthy and inspired.

OUTCOMES ASSESSED

  • 11.1 Be able to understand the nature of the Bible as enduring, trustworthy, and true and as seen throughout history.

  • 11.1.1 Explain the purpose, inspiration, and history of the Bible.

  • 11.1.2 Identify that the Bible is the the standard of truth, and archaeology gives evidence to support its credibility.

  • 11.1.3 Delineate God’s leading throughout church history in the perspective of the great controversy between Christ and Satan.

  • 11.1.4 Survey the history of Christianity, with particular reference to the Reformation (key people, events, concepts, timelines, con icts, doctrines, etc.).

  • 11.1.5 Evaluate the impact of the Reformation period on the enduring relevance and value of the Bible.

  • 11.1.6 Relate key in uences and themes of the past to present and possible future events.

  • 11.1.7 Develop techniques for studying the Bible and giving Bible studies that are biblically based.

  • RS.11.0.5 Apply a wide range of biblically based techniques for studying the Bible and incorporating its relevance from past to present; for studying Ellen White’s writings and other contemporary and historical documents.

  • RS.11.0.6 Acquire and integrate knowledge to analyze biblical stories and/or spiritual concepts, in considerable depth.

  • RS.11.0.7 Develop creative and higher order thinking skills (research, analysis, synthesis, evaluation, etc.) from a biblical perspective.

  • RS.11.0.8 Modify verbal and nonverbal responses to convey a clear, engaging, in uential message across a range of communication types about spiritual concepts to meet the needs of speci c contexts, purposes and audiences.

  • RS.11.0.9 Develop the skills to analyze and support one’s thinking and other’s perspectives and to respond with insight, in order to justify one’s own personal, biblical position.

  • RS.11.0.10 Identify and evaluate the relevance of biblical and historical themes explored and apply this to one’s life.

  • RS.11.0.11 Identify and investigate the ways in which one’s relationship with God is life-changing and is expressed through knowledge, attitudes, and actions.

  • RS.11.0.12 Learn relationship-building skills by applying Christian principles of genuine care for others, handling challenges in human relationships, resolving conflict, communicating effectively, accepting responsibility, participating in community, and serving others.
